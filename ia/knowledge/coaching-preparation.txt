SECTION 1: PERIODIZATION & TRAINING CAMPS
================================================================================

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1.1 — 8-WEEK CAMP STRUCTURE (STANDARD FIGHT CAMP)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

This is the standard preparation timeline for a scheduled bout. The fighter
should enter camp healthy, at a reasonable walking weight (within 12-15% of
target fight weight), and without significant injuries.

WEEK 1 — BASE BUILDING / ASSESSMENT
Goal: Establish aerobic foundation, assess current fitness, begin film study.
Volume: HIGH (75-80% of peak training volume)
Intensity: LOW to MODERATE (RPE 5-6 average)
Sessions:
  - 6 training days, 1 full rest day
  - 2x technical striking sessions (focus on fundamentals, not power)
  - 2x grappling/wrestling sessions (positional drilling, flow rolling)
  - 2x strength & conditioning (moderate loads, movement quality focus)
  - 3x aerobic sessions (30-45min steady state at 130-150bpm)
  - 1x film study session (study opponent footage if available)
Sparring: NONE. Technical flow only.
Strength focus: Hypertrophy/work capacity (3x8-12 at 65-75% 1RM)
Notes: Baseline HRV and body composition measurements taken. Establish sleep
and nutrition patterns. Identify any nagging issues to address with physio.

WEEK 2 — BASE BUILDING / TECHNICAL VOLUME
Goal: Increase technical volume, continue aerobic base, introduce gameplan ideas.
Volume: HIGH (80% of peak)
Intensity: MODERATE (RPE 6-7)
Sessions:
  - 6 training days, 1 rest day
  - 2x striking (begin working combinations specific to opponent)
  - 2x grappling (start working specific positions from gameplan)
  - 2x S&C (increase loads slightly, begin power development)
  - 3x aerobic (include 1 tempo run: 3min at 75%, 1min easy, x5 rounds)
  - 2x film study (detailed opponent breakdown with coaching team)
Sparring: Light technical sparring (1 session, 3-4 rounds, 60% power)
Strength focus: Transition to strength (4x5 at 78-82% 1RM)
Notes: Nutrition dialed in. Weight check — ensure trajectory is correct.
Begin identifying primary gameplan strategies (A, B, and C plans).

WEEK 3 — INTENSIFICATION
Goal: Increase intensity significantly, implement gameplan in live situations.
Volume: HIGH (85% of peak — this is near-peak volume)
Intensity: MODERATE-HIGH (RPE 7-8)
Sessions:
  - 6 training days, 1 rest day
  - 3x striking (gameplan-specific combos, counters, defense)
  - 2x grappling (live positional sparring, submission chains)
  - 1x wrestling (takedown/anti-takedown specific work)
  - 2x S&C (heavy — working toward peak strength numbers)
  - 2x conditioning (introduce fight-pace intervals: 5min rounds, 1min rest)
  - 1x film study
Sparring: 2 sessions, moderate intensity (70-80% power)
  - Session 1: 4-5 rounds standing focus
  - Session 2: 4-5 rounds mixed (start standing, allow grappling)
Strength focus: Peak strength (5x3 at 85-90% 1RM, include power cleans)
Notes: This is the hardest training week alongside Week 4. Monitor recovery
closely. HRV should not drop more than 10% from baseline consistently.

WEEK 4 — INTENSIFICATION / PEAK VOLUME
Goal: Maximum training stress. Hardest week of camp. Test gameplan under pressure.
Volume: PEAK (100% — highest volume of entire camp)
Intensity: HIGH (RPE 8-9)
Sessions:
  - 6 training days, 1 rest day (consider 2 rest days if recovery poor)
  - 3x striking (hard technical work, pad work at fight pace)
  - 2x grappling (hard live rounds, simulate scrambles)
  - 1x wrestling (live takedown rounds, 3min on/1min off)
  - 2x S&C (heavy + power: squat/deadlift heavy, then plyo work)
  - 2x conditioning (fight simulation: 5x5min at 85-90% effort)
  - 1x film study (finalize gameplan, identify key moments to exploit)
Sparring: 2 sessions, HARD (85-90% power, with protective gear)
  - Session 1: Full MMA rounds, 5 rounds, with corners
  - Session 2: Specific situational sparring (e.g., start in bad position)
Strength focus: Peak (work up to heavy singles/doubles, maintain power work)
Notes: Expect fatigue. This is intentional overreach. Fighter may feel flat.
That is acceptable — supercompensation will occur during taper.

WEEK 5 — FIGHT-SPECIFIC / EARLY TAPER
Goal: Transition from volume to quality. Everything now mimics fight conditions.
Volume: REDUCED (70-75% of peak)
Intensity: HIGH (RPE 8-9, but fewer total sets/rounds)
Sessions:
  - 5-6 training days, 1-2 rest days
  - 2x striking (fight-specific: practice exact sequences from gameplan)
  - 2x grappling (fight-specific: practice transitions and finishes)
  - 1x S&C (maintain strength only: 3x3 at 82-85%, explosive movements)
  - 2x conditioning (fight simulation rounds with rest matching fight)
  - 1x full simulation (dress rehearsal: full rounds with corner, clock, ref)
Sparring: 1-2 sessions (80-85% power, focus on executing gameplan)
  - Choose sparring partners who mimic opponent's style
Strength focus: Maintenance only. No PRs. Just maintain neural patterns.
Notes: Begin mild caloric reduction if weight cut needed. Sleep prioritized
(8-9 hours). Reduce outside stressors. Visualization training begins (3x/week).

WEEK 6 — PEAK PERFORMANCE / SHARPENING
Goal: Peak power output, sharpen timing, fine-tune gameplan details.
Volume: REDUCED (60-65% of peak)
Intensity: VERY HIGH for short bursts (RPE 9-10 in intervals, low otherwise)
Sessions:
  - 5 training days, 2 rest days
  - 2x striking (short sharp pad rounds, reaction drills, timing work)
  - 1x grappling (flow + specific submissions/escapes)
  - 1x S&C (power only: jump squats, med ball, explosive pulls — low volume)
  - 2x conditioning (short high-intensity: 30s max/30s rest x8-10)
  - 1x light simulation (3 rounds, focus on round 1 start + between-round routine)
Sparring: 1 session MAX (controlled, 70-75% power, 3 rounds only)
  LAST HARD SPARRING SESSION OF CAMP — after this, no more hard sparring.
Strength focus: Power/speed (40-60% load moved explosively, 3x3)
Notes: Fighter should start feeling sharp, fast, and fresh. Visualization daily.
Weight cut water loading assessment. Plan exact cut timeline.

WEEK 7 — TAPER
Goal: Full recovery while maintaining sharpness. Reduce volume 50% from peak.
Volume: LOW (50% of peak volume)
Intensity: MODERATE (RPE 6-7, just enough to stay activated)
Sessions:
  - 4-5 training days, 2-3 rest days
  - 2x light technical work (shadow boxing, drilling, mitt work at 50-60%)
  - 1x light grappling (flow rolling, technique review, no intensity)
  - 1x S&C (light activation: bodyweight + bands, mobility focus)
  - 1x conditioning (20min easy aerobic + short burst: 4x20s sprint)
  - Daily: visualization (10min), breathing exercises, mental preparation
Sparring: ZERO. No sparring this week.
Strength focus: None. Light movement only.
Notes: Begin water loading protocol if cutting weight. Reduce fiber intake.
Sleep 9+ hours. Limit social media and external stimuli. Review gameplan
mentally but do not add new information. Confidence-building conversations
with coaching team. Address any remaining anxiety or doubts.

WEEK 8 — FIGHT WEEK
Goal: Make weight safely, stay activated without fatigue, peak mentally.
Volume: MINIMAL (20-25% of peak)
Intensity: LOW with brief activation bursts
Sessions:
  MONDAY: 20min light technical work + visualization + weight cut protocol
  TUESDAY: 15min shadow boxing, stretching, sauna if needed for weight
  WEDNESDAY: Light 10min pad work (just to stay sharp), cut water
  THURSDAY: Activation only (5min shadow, 5min stretch), final weight cut
  FRIDAY (WEIGH-IN DAY): Weigh in → immediate rehydration → light meal → rest
  SATURDAY (FIGHT DAY): Morning activation routine (shadow box 2x2min, dynamic
    warmup), eat scheduled meals, arrive at venue 3h early, warmup with coach
    (pads, movement, 15-20min), mental cues, compete.
  OR if Friday fight: adjust timeline accordingly.
Notes: EVERYTHING is about making weight and peaking mentally. Do not learn
anything new. Do not change the gameplan. Trust the preparation. The work
is done — fight week is about delivery, not preparation.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1.2 — 12-WEEK CAMP STRUCTURE (TITLE FIGHT / MAJOR EVENT)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Used for championship bouts, tournament finals, or when a fighter needs extra
preparation time (new opponent style, coming back from injury, moving weight
classes).

WEEKS 1-3: FOUNDATION PHASE
Goal: Build work capacity, address weaknesses, begin skill development for
specific opponent challenges.
Volume: Moderate-High (65-75% of eventual peak)
Intensity: Low-Moderate (RPE 5-7)
  - Focus on identified weaknesses from previous fights
  - If opponent is a wrestler: extra wrestling 3x/week
  - If opponent is a striker: extra defensive boxing/head movement work
  - Strength: hypertrophy block (3-4x8-12 reps, progressive overload)
  - Conditioning: primarily aerobic base (long steady-state, hiking, swimming)
  - Sparring: Light technical only, 1x/week, focused on specific skills
  - Film study: Deep dive on opponent (all available fights, patterns, habits)
  - Mobility/prehab: address any imbalances, build joint resilience

WEEKS 4-6: DEVELOPMENT PHASE
Goal: Build specific fitness, develop gameplan, increase sparring intensity.
Volume: High (80-90% of peak)
Intensity: Moderate-High (RPE 7-8)
  - Gameplan now established — drill specific sequences daily
  - Strength: transition to max strength (4-5x3-5 at 82-90% 1RM)
  - Conditioning: introduce sport-specific intervals alongside aerobic
  - Sparring: 2x/week, moderate to hard (75-85% power), 4-6 rounds
  - Begin working specific scenarios (what if taken down? what if pressured?)
  - Bring in training partners who mimic opponent's style and body type

WEEKS 7-9: INTENSIFICATION PHASE
Goal: Peak training stress. Maximize fight-specific fitness and skill execution.
Volume: PEAK (100%)
Intensity: High (RPE 8-9)
  - This is the hardest 3-week block. Intentional overreach.
  - Strength: peak (heavy singles/doubles/triples)
  - Conditioning: fight simulation dominates (5x5min rounds multiple times/week)
  - Sparring: 2-3x/week, hard (85-90% power), full rounds with corners
  - Full fight simulations with referee, clock, walk-out, everything
  - Mental toughness work: train tired, simulate adversity
  - Weight: check weekly, ensure trajectory is correct for fight week

WEEKS 10-11: SHARPENING / TAPER
Goal: Recovery + maintain peak qualities. Reduce volume dramatically.
Volume: Declining (Week 10: 65%, Week 11: 45% of peak)
Intensity: Moderate-High but brief
  - Sparring: Week 10 — last hard session (1 session, 4 rounds)
  - Sparring: Week 11 — light or none
  - Strength: maintenance only (2x/week, light loads, explosive)
  - Conditioning: short + sharp (quality over quantity)
  - Technical: refine, don't add. Practice finishes, starts, transitions.
  - Mental: daily visualization, routine establishment, confidence building

WEEK 12: FIGHT WEEK
  - Same protocol as Week 8 in the standard camp (see above)
  - Extra emphasis on mental readiness given the magnitude of the fight
  - Championship rounds preparation: remind fighter about pace for 5 rounds

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1.3 — WEEKLY TEMPLATE (IN-CAMP, PEAK WEEKS)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

MONDAY — STRENGTH + STRIKING
  AM (60min): Strength & Conditioning
    - Warmup: 10min (bike/row + dynamic mobility)
    - Main lifts: Squat or Deadlift 4x4 at 85%, Bench or OHP 4x4
    - Accessory: Pull-ups 3x8, RDLs 3x8, Core 3x30s
    - Cooldown: 5min stretch
  PM (90min): Striking Technical
    - Warmup: 15min shadow boxing + footwork drills
    - Pad work: 6x3min rounds (gameplan combinations)
    - Heavy bag: 3x3min (power shots)
    - Partner drills: 20min (specific defensive/offensive sequences)
    - Cool down: 10min stretching + foam roll

TUESDAY — GRAPPLING + CONDITIONING
  AM (60min): Wrestling/Takedown Practice
    - Warmup: 15min (wrestling-specific: sprawls, shots, pummeling)
    - Technique: 20min (drilling 2-3 takedown setups/defenses)
    - Live rounds: 5x3min (start standing, reset if taken down)
    - Cool down: 10min
  PM (75min): Grappling + Conditioning Finisher
    - BJJ/Grappling: 45min (technique 20min + positional sparring 25min)
    - Conditioning: 20min (4x4min rounds at fight pace, 1min rest between)
    - Cool down: 10min

WEDNESDAY — ACTIVE RECOVERY
  AM (45min): Recovery Session
    - 20min light aerobic (bike or swim at <130bpm)
    - 15min mobility/yoga flow
    - 10min foam rolling
  PM: Film study (30-45min) + visualization (10min)
  Optional: massage or physiotherapy session

THURSDAY — SPARRING + POWER
  AM (45min): Power Development
    - Warmup: 10min
    - Plyometrics: Box jumps 4x5, Med ball throws 3x6, Broad jumps 3x5
    - Olympic lifts: Power clean 5x3 at 70-75%
    - Cool down: stretch
  PM (90min): MMA Sparring
    - Warmup: 20min (shadow, pads, movement)
    - Sparring: 5-6 rounds x 5min (match fight conditions)
    - Post-sparring review: 15min (immediate feedback with coach)
    - Cool down: 15min (ice bath optional)

FRIDAY — TECHNICAL + CONDITIONING
  AM (60min): Striking Technical (light)
    - Pad work: 4x3min (speed and accuracy focus)
    - Defense drills: 15min (slipping, checking, blocking)
    - Body work: 10min (specific body shot sequences)
  PM (60min): Conditioning
    - Fight simulation: 5x5min rounds at 80-85% effort, 1min rest
    - OR interval session: 10x30s all-out / 30s rest + 3x3min tempo
    - Cool down: 15min easy + stretch

SATURDAY — GRAPPLING FOCUS
  AM (90min): Full Grappling Session
    - Warmup: 15min (flow roll)
    - Technique: 30min (specific submissions, escapes, transitions)
    - Live rolling: 6x5min rounds (vary partners for different styles)
    - Cool down: 10min
  PM: Light conditioning (20min easy cardio) OR full rest

SUNDAY — COMPLETE REST
  No training. Sleep, eat well, mental recovery.
  Optional: light walk, meditation, film study if desired.
  MANDATORY: Minimum 8 hours sleep.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1.4 — TRAINING LOAD MANAGEMENT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

RPE SCALE FOR MMA:
  1-2: Very light, barely any effort (slow shadow boxing, walking)
  3-4: Light, easy conversation possible (technical drilling, flow roll)
  5-6: Moderate, conversation in short sentences (pad work at 50-60%)
  7-8: Hard, cannot sustain conversation (sparring, hard rounds)
  9: Very hard, near maximum effort (fight simulation, hard sparring)
  10: Maximum effort (all-out intervals, fight-ending sequences)

MONITORING OVERTRAINING:
Signs to watch for:
  - Resting heart rate elevated 5+ bpm above baseline for 3+ consecutive days
  - HRV (Heart Rate Variability) dropped 15%+ from rolling 7-day average
  - Sleep quality declining despite adequate time in bed
  - Mood disturbance: irritability, lack of motivation, feeling of dread
  - Performance drops: power output declining, slower reaction time
  - Appetite changes: either no appetite or excessive hunger
  - Increased illness frequency (sore throat, cold symptoms)
  - Joint pain that does not improve with warmup
  - Persistent muscle soreness >72 hours after session

When these appear, implement:
  - Reduce volume by 30-40% for 3-5 days
  - Increase sleep by 1 hour
  - Add caloric intake (200-300kcal from carbs)
  - Prioritize parasympathetic activities (walking, meditation, easy swim)
  - If not resolved in 5 days: take 2-3 full rest days

DELOAD WEEKS:
When: Every 3-4 weeks of hard training (in 12-week camp: end of week 3, 6, 9)
How:
  - Reduce training volume by 40-50%
  - Reduce intensity by 20-30%
  - Maintain frequency (still train same days, just less per session)
  - Focus on technique quality, not output
  - Extra recovery: massage, sleep, nutrition quality
Duration: 3-5 days minimum, up to 7 days if significantly fatigued.

VOLUME VS INTENSITY BALANCE:
  - Cannot have both at maximum simultaneously for more than 2 weeks
  - Early camp: high volume, moderate intensity (build capacity)
  - Mid camp: high both (intentional overreach, brief: 2-3 weeks max)
  - Late camp: low volume, high intensity (sharpen and peak)
  - Fight week: minimal both (just enough to stay activated)

SECTION 2: STRENGTH & CONDITIONING FOR MMA
================================================================================

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2.1 — STRENGTH TRAINING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

PRIMARY EXERCISES (Compound Movements):
  Squat (back squat, front squat): Foundation of lower body power. Builds leg
    drive for takedowns, takedown defense, striking power from the ground up.
    Programming: 4x4-6 at 80-87% 1RM during strength phase.

  Deadlift (conventional, sumo, trap bar): Posterior chain king. Hip hinge
    power for clinch work, lifting opponents, scramble explosiveness.
    Programming: Work up to heavy triple or 5x3 at 82-88% 1RM.

  Bench Press (flat, incline, close grip): Upper body pushing. Relevant for
    framing, push-off from bottom, creating distance.
    Programming: 4x5 at 78-85% 1RM. Close grip for tricep emphasis.

  Overhead Press (strict, push press): Shoulder strength for clinch, frames,
    defending takedowns with underhooks.
    Programming: 3x5 at 78-82% 1RM strict, or 5x3 push press for power.

  Power Clean: Full body explosive movement. Trains triple extension crucial
    for striking power and takedown explosiveness.
    Programming: 5x3 at 70-78% 1RM. Technique is paramount — never grind reps.

  Snatch (hang snatch, power snatch): Rate of force development, full body
    coordination, overhead stability.
    Programming: 5x2 at 65-75% 1RM. Only if technically proficient.

  Kettlebell Work: Swings (hip hinge power, conditioning), Turkish get-ups
    (shoulder stability, ground-to-standing movement), goblet squats (mobility).
    Programming: Swings 5x10-15, TGU 3x2 each side, as accessory or warmup.

REP SCHEMES FOR FIGHTERS:
  Maximum Strength: 3-5 sets of 1-5 reps at 85-95% 1RM. Rest 3-5min.
    When: Early-mid camp, 2-3x/week. Builds force production ceiling.
  Power: 4-6 sets of 2-3 reps at 70-80% 1RM (moved as fast as possible).
    When: Mid-late camp. Converts strength to usable speed-strength.
  Hypertrophy: 3-4 sets of 8-12 reps at 65-75% 1RM. Rest 60-90s.
    When: Off-season or early camp weeks 1-2 only. Build muscle base.
  Strength-Endurance: 3 sets of 12-20 reps at 50-60% 1RM.
    When: Off-season only, for connective tissue health and work capacity.

WHEN TO LIFT IN CAMP:
  Weeks 1-4: Heavy lifting 2-3x/week. Progressive overload still applies.
  Weeks 5-6: Reduce to 2x/week. Maintain loads but reduce volume (fewer sets).
  Weeks 7-8: 1x/week max. Light weight, explosive speed. Maintenance only.
  Fight week: No lifting. At most, light activation (push-ups, bands).

COMMON PROGRAMS ADAPTED FOR FIGHTERS:

  Westside for Fighters (Conjugate):
    - Max effort day: work up to 1-3RM on main lift (rotate exercise weekly)
    - Dynamic effort day: 8-10x2-3 at 50-60% 1RM with bands/chains, maximum speed
    - Accessory work: target weaknesses, keep volume moderate
    - Perfect for fighters: develops both max strength AND speed-strength

  5/3/1 for Fighters (Wendler adaptation):
    - Week 1: 3x5 (65/75/85%)
    - Week 2: 3x3 (70/80/90%)
    - Week 3: 5/3/1 (75/85/95%)
    - Week 4: Deload (40/50/60%)
    - Reduce supplemental work vs standard 5/3/1. Skip BBB template.
    - 2 days/week (upper/lower split) during camp.

  Simple Strength for Fighters (Minimalist):
    - Squat 3x5, Deadlift 3x3, Bench 3x5, Rows 3x8, twice per week
    - Add 2.5kg/week until plateau, then reset 10% and rebuild
    - Perfect for fighters who cannot dedicate much time to S&C

2.2 — POWER & EXPLOSIVENESS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

PLYOMETRICS:
  Box Jumps: 4-5 sets of 3-5 reps. Step down (don't jump down). Focus on
    maximal height. Trains rate of force development.
  Depth Jumps: 3 sets of 5. Drop from 30-50cm box, immediately jump max height
    upon landing. Advanced exercise — only if squat >1.5x bodyweight.
  Broad Jumps: 4x5. Maximum horizontal distance. Rest fully between sets.
  Med Ball Throws: Chest pass, overhead slam, rotational throw, scoop toss.
    3-4 sets of 5-6 reps each direction. Highly sport-specific for MMA.
  Single Leg Bounds: 3x5 each leg. Unilateral power for kicks, level changes.

OLYMPIC LIFTS:
  Power Clean: 5x3 at 70-78% 1RM. Full hip extension = striking power.
  Hang Snatch: 4x3 at 65-72% 1RM. Overhead rate of force development.
  Clean Pull: 4x3 at 90-100% clean 1RM. Overloads the pull without catch.
  Push Jerk: 3x3 at 78-85% 1RM. Full body power expression.
  NOTE: Only prescribe if technically proficient. Bad Olympic lifts = injury.

BALLISTIC TRAINING:
  Jump Squats: 5x5 at 30-40% back squat 1RM. Maximum bar speed on every rep.
  Bench Throws (Smith machine): 5x5 at 30-40% bench 1RM. Release at top.
  Explosive Push-ups (clap push-ups): 4x6-8. Upper body rate of force.
  Band-Resisted Movements: Squat with bands, bench with bands — accommodating
    resistance teaches acceleration through full range of motion.

SPORT-SPECIFIC POWER:
  Striking Power Drills:
    - Heavy bag: 5x10s maximum power (all-out, rest 50s between)
    - Sledgehammer slams: 4x10 each side (rotational power)
    - Landmine press/rotation: 3x8 each side (punch-specific movement pattern)
    - Resistance band punches: 3x20 each hand (speed against resistance)
  Explosive Hip Movement:
    - Hip escapes with band resistance: 3x10 each side
    - Explosive bridging: 4x8 (hip thrust with pause at bottom, explode up)
    - Sprawl to shot: 4x6 (defensive to offensive transition speed)
    - Level change with medicine ball: 3x8 (simulate shot entry with load)

2.3 — CARDIO & CONDITIONING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

ENERGY SYSTEMS OVERVIEW:
  Phosphocreatine System (ATP-PC): 0-10 seconds
    - Fuels: explosive strikes, takedown shots, scrambles, finish attempts
    - Training: all-out sprints 6-10s, full recovery (1:10 work:rest ratio)
    - Example: 8x8s bike sprint / 80s rest

  Glycolytic System (Anaerobic): 10 seconds - 2 minutes
    - Fuels: sustained high-intensity exchanges, pressure sequences, chain wrestling
    - Training: 30s-2min intervals at 85-95% effort, incomplete recovery
    - Example: 8x30s / 30s rest or 5x90s / 90s rest

  Oxidative System (Aerobic): 2+ minutes
    - Fuels: ability to recover between exchanges, sustain pace across rounds
    - Training: sustained effort 20-60min at 60-75% max HR, or longer intervals
    - Example: 30min run at 130-150bpm, or 5x4min / 2min rest at 75-80%

  MMA REQUIRES ALL THREE: A 5-minute round includes bursts (ATP-PC), sustained
  exchanges (glycolytic), and recovery periods (aerobic). A fighter with poor
  aerobic base cannot recover between bursts. A fighter with no anaerobic
  capacity cannot sustain pressure. A fighter without phosphocreatine cannot
  generate knockout power in the 4th round.

SPECIFIC PROTOCOLS:

  Cardiac Output Method (Aerobic Base):
    - Intensity: 130-150bpm (conversational pace)
    - Duration: 30-60 minutes continuous
    - Modality: running, cycling, swimming, rowing, assault bike
    - Frequency: 3-5x/week in base phase, 1-2x/week in camp
    - Purpose: builds cardiac stroke volume, capillary density, mitochondria
    - This is the FOUNDATION. Without it, all other conditioning fails long-term.

  Tempo Intervals (Threshold / Lactate Work):
    - Intensity: 70-80% max effort (hard but sustainable)
    - Duration: 3-5 minutes work, 1-2 minutes recovery
    - Sets: 4-6 rounds
    - Purpose: raise lactate threshold, sustain higher pace without fatigue
    - Example: 5x4min assault bike at 80% / 90s easy between

  Fight Simulation (Sport-Specific):
    - Duration: 5-minute rounds, 1-minute rest (matches 3 or 5 round fight)
    - Intensity: 80-90% effort (match actual fight intensity)
    - Sets: 3 rounds (3-round fight sim) or 5 rounds (championship sim)
    - Modality: pads/bag/grappling dummy/partner drills (NOT just cardio machine)
    - Purpose: specific metabolic conditioning under sport conditions
    - Frequency: 2-3x/week during peak camp weeks

  High Intensity Intervals (Anaerobic Power):
    - 30/30: 30s all-out / 30s active rest, x10-15 rounds
    - 60/60: 60s hard / 60s rest, x8-10 rounds
    - 10/50: 10s MAX sprint / 50s rest, x10-12 (phosphocreatine repeat)
    - Purpose: push anaerobic ceiling higher, tolerate more lactate
    - Frequency: 2-3x/week during intensification phase

  Tabata (Maximum Oxygen Debt):
    - 20 seconds ALL-OUT / 10 seconds rest, x8 rounds (4 minutes total)
    - Modality: bike, assault bike, or bodyweight (burpees, thrusters)
    - Purpose: maximal oxygen deficit, push VO2max
    - WARNING: brutally fatiguing. Use 1-2x/week max. Never on sparring days.

HEART RATE ZONES:
  Zone 1 (50-60% max HR): Active recovery, warmups
  Zone 2 (60-70% max HR): Aerobic base building (cardiac output work)
  Zone 3 (70-80% max HR): Tempo/threshold work (lactate management)
  Zone 4 (80-90% max HR): Anaerobic intervals (fight pace)
  Zone 5 (90-100% max HR): Maximum effort (short bursts only)

  To estimate max HR: 220 - age (rough) or use field test (best option).

WEEKLY CARDIO PROGRAMMING IN CAMP:
  Early camp (weeks 1-3): Zone 2 x3-4, Zone 3 x1-2, Zone 4 x1
  Mid camp (weeks 4-6): Zone 2 x2, Zone 3 x1-2, Zone 4 x2-3
  Late camp (weeks 7-8): Zone 2 x1 (recovery), Zone 4-5 x1-2 (sharpening)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2.4 — MOBILITY & INJURY PREVENTION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

ESSENTIAL STRETCHES FOR FIGHTERS:
  Hip Flexors: Half-kneeling hip flexor stretch, couch stretch, pigeon pose
    WHY: Tight from sitting, limits kick height and takedown defense stance.
  Shoulders: Sleeper stretch, cross-body stretch, doorway pec stretch, band pull-aparts
    WHY: Tight from punching/clinch work. Limits reach and causes impingement.
  Ankles: Wall ankle mobilization, banded ankle distraction
    WHY: Limits squat depth, stance width, and lateral movement.
  Thoracic Spine: Foam roller extensions, open book rotations, cat-cow
    WHY: Rounded upper back from guard/clinch. Limits rotation for striking.
  Hamstrings: Romanian deadlift stretch, standing toe touch, PNF stretching
    WHY: Tight from sprawling, limits high kicks and guard flexibility.
  Groin/Adductors: Side-lying adductor stretch, frog stretch, Cossack squat
    WHY: Crucial for guard retention, kick flexibility, stance width.

FOAM ROLLING PROTOCOL (10-15 minutes):
  Order: Calves (60s each) → Quads (60s each) → IT band (30s each) →
  Glutes (60s each) → Upper back (60s) → Lats (30s each)
  Technique: Slow rolling, pause on tender spots for 20-30s.
  When: Pre-training (brief, 5min) or post-training / evening (full protocol).

DYNAMIC WARMUP ROUTINE (Pre-Training, 10-15 min):
  1. General: 3min light jog or jump rope
  2. Leg swings: 10 forward/back + 10 side-to-side each leg
  3. Walking lunges with rotation: 10 each side
  4. Inchworms: 8 reps
  5. World's greatest stretch: 5 each side
  6. High knees: 20m
  7. Butt kicks: 20m
  8. Lateral shuffles: 20m each direction
  9. Arm circles: 10 forward + 10 backward (small then large)
  10. Sport-specific: shadow boxing 2min at 40-50% effort

COOL DOWN ROUTINE (Post-Training, 10 min):
  1. 3-5min easy movement (walking, light bike)
  2. Static stretching: hold each position 30-45s (hip flexors, hamstrings,
     shoulders, thoracic rotation, groin)
  3. Deep breathing: 10 breaths (4s inhale, 6s exhale — activate parasympathetic)

COMMON IMBALANCES IN FIGHTERS AND CORRECTIONS:
  Internal rotation dominance (shoulders): From punching/clinch
    Fix: External rotation exercises (band pull-aparts, face pulls, prone Y-raises)
    Protocol: 3x15 daily as warmup

  Anterior pelvic tilt / tight hip flexors: From stance + sprawling
    Fix: Glute bridges, hip flexor stretching, dead bugs for anterior core
    Protocol: Stretch 2x30s + 2x15 glute bridges daily

  Weak posterior chain relative to quads: From heavy squat/sprint focus
    Fix: Romanian deadlifts, Nordic curls, hip thrusts
    Protocol: 3x8-12 twice per week

  Rounded thoracic spine: From guard/clinch/computer posture
    Fix: Foam roller extensions, prone Y-T-W raises, face pulls
    Protocol: Foam roller 2min + 3x10 prone raises daily

  Weak neck: Critical for concussion prevention in MMA
    Fix: Neck curls (front/back/side), manual resistance, neck harness
    Protocol: 3x15-20 each direction, 3x/week. Build gradually.

SECTION 3: WEIGHT CUTTING — COMPLETE GUIDE
================================================================================

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
3.1 — UNDERSTANDING WEIGHT CLASSES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

WHY FIGHTERS CUT WEIGHT:
Fighters cut weight to compete at a lower weight class where they will have a
size and strength advantage over opponents who are naturally smaller. On fight
night (24-32 hours after weigh-in), a fighter may be 10-15% heavier than the
class limit. This is a competitive advantage: more mass = more force = more
damage resistance.

SAFE RANGE:
  - 8-10% of walking weight is a manageable cut for experienced fighters
  - Example: walking weight 77kg, fighting at 70kg (10% cut)
  - This allows full recovery and performance maintenance

MODERATE RISK RANGE:
  - 10-12% cut is aggressive and requires precise protocol
  - Experienced fighters with good discipline can manage this
  - Performance risk: may not fully rehydrate, speed/endurance compromised

DANGEROUS RANGE:
  - >12% cut is dangerous and should be strongly discouraged
  - Health risks: kidney damage, cardiac arrhythmia, cognitive impairment
  - Performance is almost always compromised (slower, weaker, less durable chin)

RECOMMENDATION: A fighter should be within 6-8% of their fight weight at 4 weeks
out from a fight. If more than 10% away at 4 weeks, consider moving up a class.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
3.2 — 4-WEEK WEIGHT CUT PROTOCOL (DETAILED)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Assumes: Fighter needs to lose 6-10% bodyweight for weigh-in.
Example: Fighter walks at 76-77kg, needs to make 70kg.

WEEK 4 (28-21 days out):
  Starting weight: ~76-77kg
  Actions:
    - Establish baseline: weigh daily (AM, after bathroom, before food/water)
    - Nutrition: eat at maintenance calories with clean foods
    - Water intake: 3-4L/day (normal)
    - Training: normal camp intensity (not modified for weight yet)
    - Assess: determine how much is fat loss vs water manipulation needed
    - If >5kg over: begin mild caloric deficit (-300 to -500kcal/day)
    - Target: lose 1-1.5kg of actual body mass this week through diet alone
  End of week target: ~75-76kg

WEEK 3 (21-14 days out):
  Starting weight: ~75-76kg
  Actions:
    - Reduce caloric intake by 500kcal from maintenance
    - Protein STAYS HIGH: 2.5g/kg to preserve muscle
    - Carbs reduced: 2-3g/kg (lower end on non-training days)
    - Fat: 0.8-1g/kg
    - Water intake: increase to 6L/day (begin establishing high water baseline)
    - Sodium: normal (do not restrict yet)
    - Begin cutting empty calories, alcohol (should already be zero), sugar
    - Fiber: keep moderate this week (switch to low fiber next week)
    - Training: normal but monitor energy levels
  End of week target: ~74-75kg

WEEK 2 (14-7 days out):
  Starting weight: ~74-75kg
  Actions:
    - WATER LOADING BEGINS: 7-8L/day (this upregulates kidney excretion)
    - SODIUM LOADING: 5-8g/day added sodium (salt food heavily, add salt to water)
    - This combination trains kidneys to excrete maximum fluid constantly
    - Caloric intake: deficit of 500-750kcal from maintenance
    - Carbs: reduce to 1-2g/kg (carbs hold 3g water per 1g glycogen stored)
    - Protein: maintain 2.5g/kg
    - Low fiber diet: white rice over brown, no beans, minimal vegetables
    - Avoid gas-producing foods: cruciferous vegetables, legumes
    - Training: reduce volume (taper phase) — no heavy sweating sessions yet
    - Continue weighing daily, track fluid intake precisely
  End of week target: ~73-74kg (NOTE: water loading may keep weight elevated,
    this is expected and planned — the drop comes when water is cut)

WEEK 1 (7-0 days out) — THE CRITICAL WEEK:

  MONDAY (6 days out):
    - Water: 8L throughout the day
    - Sodium: HIGH (8g added salt)
    - Food: low carb, moderate protein, low fiber meals
    - Training: light technical work only (no sweat sessions)
    - Weight: ~73-74kg (still water loaded — this is fine)

  TUESDAY (5 days out):
    - Water: 8L
    - Sodium: HIGH (8g)
    - Food: same as Monday
    - Training: light shadow boxing, visualization
    - Weight: ~73-74kg

  WEDNESDAY (4 days out):
    - Water: CUT TO 4L (kidneys still dumping at 8L rate = net fluid loss)
    - Sodium: CUT COMPLETELY (zero added salt, low-sodium foods only)
    - Food: very low carb (<50g total), lean protein, small portions
    - Training: 10min light movement only
    - Weight: should start dropping noticeably (target ~72-73kg by evening)

  THURSDAY (3 days out):
    - Water: CUT TO 2L (spread across whole day in sips)
    - Sodium: ZERO
    - Food: minimal — small portion of lean protein, no carbs
    - Training: none or 5min shadow boxing (activation only)
    - Sauna: first session if needed (15-20min, do not exceed)
    - Monitoring: check urine color (if clear = still losing water well)
    - Weight: target ~71-72kg by evening

  FRIDAY — WEIGH-IN DAY:
    - Water: sip only (maximum 500ml throughout morning if needed)
    - Food: nothing until after weigh-in
    - Morning weight check: assess distance from target
    - If within 0.5kg of limit: just wait, may lose through breathing/sweating
    - If 0.5-1.5kg over: hot bath (20-30min) or sauna (15min sessions with breaks)
    - If >1.5kg over: This is a problem. Sauna protocol needed.
      Sauna protocol: 15min in, 5min out (weigh between), repeat until done.
      Maximum sauna time: 45-60min total. Beyond this = serious health risk.
    - WEIGH-IN: make weight, smile, take photos, begin recovery immediately.

  POST-WEIGH-IN — REHYDRATION BEGINS IMMEDIATELY:
    (See detailed rehydration protocol in section 3.4)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
3.3 — NUTRITION DURING CUT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

MACRONUTRIENT TARGETS (Final 2 weeks):
  Protein: 2.5g/kg lean body mass (muscle preservation is critical)
  Carbs: Progressively reduce from 3g/kg to <1g/kg in final 3 days
  Fat: 0.8-1g/kg (sufficient for hormone function)
  Fiber: <15g/day in final week (reduces gut weight by 0.5-1kg)

FOODS TO EAT:
  Proteins: Chicken breast, white fish (tilapia, cod), egg whites, lean turkey,
    whey protein isolate
  Carbs (when allowed): White rice (low fiber), white bread, rice cakes
  Fats: Small amounts of olive oil, egg yolks, avocado (in moderation)
  Minimal residue: These foods leave minimal waste in the GI tract

FOODS TO AVOID:
  - Dairy (bloating, water retention, slow digestion)
  - High-fiber foods (oats, brown rice, whole grains, legumes, most vegetables)
  - Sodium-rich foods in final 3 days (no soy sauce, processed food, canned food)
  - Cruciferous vegetables (broccoli, cauliflower — gas and bloating)
  - Carbonated beverages
  - Alcohol (dehydrating in wrong way, liver stress, poor recovery)
  - Artificial sweeteners (can cause bloating)

SUPPLEMENTS DURING CUT:
  - Electrolytes (early in cut ONLY, stop when sodium is cut): potassium,
    magnesium, sodium in early loading phase
  - Magnesium glycinate: 400-600mg/day (prevents cramps, aids sleep)
  - Potassium: 200-400mg/day (early in cut, stop 3 days before weigh-in)
  - Dandelion root extract: mild natural diuretic (controversial, some use it)
  - DO NOT USE pharmaceutical diuretics (dangerous, banned in many jurisdictions)
  - Vitamin C: 1000mg/day (mild diuretic effect + immune support)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
3.4 — REHYDRATION PROTOCOL (POST-WEIGH-IN TO FIGHT)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

TIMING: Assume weigh-in is Friday afternoon, fight is Saturday evening.
That gives approximately 20-28 hours to recover.

FIRST 30 MINUTES AFTER WEIGH-IN:
  - 500ml oral rehydration solution (Pedialyte, WHO-ORS, or similar)
  - Contains sodium, potassium, glucose for optimal absorption
  - Sip steadily, do not chug (stomach may be sensitive)
  - NO solid food yet

30-60 MINUTES POST-WEIGH-IN:
  - Additional 500ml water or diluted sports drink
  - Small easy-to-digest food: banana, white rice (small portion: 150g)
  - Continue sipping fluids between bites

1-2 HOURS POST-WEIGH-IN:
  - First full meal: 200g white rice + 150g chicken breast + small sweet potato
  - Continue drinking: total 1-1.5L consumed by this point
  - Electrolyte supplement with this meal (magnesium + sodium + potassium)

2-6 HOURS POST-WEIGH-IN:
  - Eat every 2 hours: easily digestible meals
  - Meal options: pasta + lean meat, rice + fish, sandwich on white bread
  - Total fluid target: 3-4L by bedtime
  - Avoid heavy fats (slow digestion), heavy fiber, large single meals
  - Better: 4-5 smaller meals than 2 huge ones

BEDTIME:
  - One more small snack: protein + carbs (yogurt + honey, protein shake + banana)
  - Magnesium supplement for sleep (400mg)
  - Keep water by bed, sip if waking during night

FIGHT DAY MORNING:
  - Weigh yourself: goal is to have regained 5-7% bodyweight
    (Example: weighed in at 70kg, now should be 73.5-75kg)
  - Breakfast: familiar, well-tolerated meal (oats + banana + eggs, or rice + eggs)
  - Continue hydrating: 1-1.5L before noon
  - Avoid anything new or unusual (no new foods, no experiments)

3-4 HOURS BEFORE FIGHT:
  - Last meal: moderate portion of carbs + protein (pasta + chicken, rice + fish)
  - No heavy foods, no high fat, no high fiber
  - Small amount of caffeine acceptable (coffee or pre-workout)

1-2 HOURS BEFORE FIGHT:
  - Stop eating
  - Small sips of water or sports drink only
  - Light snack if needed: banana or handful of gummy bears (fast sugar)

WARNING SIGNS DURING REHYDRATION:
  - Cannot keep fluids down (vomiting): seek medical attention
  - Severe muscle cramps that do not resolve: electrolyte emergency
  - Confusion or disorientation: stop cut, seek medical help
  - Heart palpitations or chest pain: EMERGENCY, immediate medical attention
  - Dark brown urine that does not improve after 2L fluid: kidney concern

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
3.5 — DANGERS & RED FLAGS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

KIDNEY DAMAGE:
  Repeated aggressive cuts (multiple times per year) cause cumulative kidney
  stress. Each severe dehydration episode forces kidneys to concentrate urine
  beyond normal capacity. Chronic repeated dehydration is linked to kidney
  stones and reduced kidney function over career span.

CARDIAC RISKS:
  Severe dehydration thickens blood, reduces plasma volume, forces heart to
  work harder at lower output. Electrolyte imbalances (low potassium, low
  magnesium) can cause cardiac arrhythmias. Combined with the extreme physical
  exertion of a fight, this creates genuine cardiac event risk.

COGNITIVE IMPAIRMENT:
  Brain is 75% water. Even 3% dehydration impairs reaction time, decision-making,
  and coordination. A severely dehydrated fighter entering a fight with degraded
  cognitive function is at increased risk of being knocked out (brain more
  vulnerable to impact when dehydrated).

SIGNS TO STOP IMMEDIATELY:
  - Urine is dark brown (rhabdomyolysis risk)
  - Dizziness when standing that does not resolve
  - Heart palpitations at rest
  - Confusion, inability to answer simple questions
  - Loss of consciousness (even brief)
  - Seizure activity
  - Inability to produce ANY urine for 8+ hours

DEATHS IN COMBAT SPORTS FROM WEIGHT CUTTING:
  - Yang Jian Bing (2015): MMA fighter, died from weight cut complications
    during ONE Championship event preparation.
  - Jessica Lindsay (2019): Muay Thai fighter, died from heat stroke during
    extreme sauna use for weight cut.
  - Multiple amateur wrestling deaths in late 1990s prompted NCAA rule changes.
  - Ramon Dekkers-era Muay Thai: several fighters hospitalized from kidney
    failure following extreme cuts in Thailand heat.
  These deaths are PREVENTABLE with proper protocols and the willingness
  to move up a weight class rather than risk health.

SECTION 4: MENTAL PREPARATION
================================================================================

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
4.1 — PRE-FIGHT MENTAL PROTOCOL (WEEK-BY-WEEK)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

4 WEEKS OUT:
  - Begin visualization training: 3 sessions per week, 10 minutes each
  - Establish "focus words" — 2-3 words that represent fight identity
    (e.g., "Pressure. Relentless. Finish.")
  - Journal: write down fears/anxieties about the fight. Externalize them.
  - Identify personal "why" for this fight (motivation anchoring)
  - Begin reducing unnecessary social commitments

3 WEEKS OUT:
  - Visualization increases to 4x/week
  - Practice pre-performance routine (same warmup sequence every training session)
  - Begin breath work practice: 5 minutes daily (box breathing or 4-7-8 pattern)
  - Discuss scenarios with coach: "What if I get taken down early?" etc.
  - Prepare answers mentally for every possible in-fight situation

2 WEEKS OUT:
  - DAILY visualization: 10 minutes minimum
  - Establish mantras for specific fight moments:
    * Getting hurt: "Breathe. Survive. Reset."
    * Feeling tired: "Push now. Rest is coming."
    * Winning moment: "Finish. Don't celebrate early."
  - Breathing practice: 10 minutes daily
  - Reduce external stimuli: limit social media, news, unnecessary conversations
  - Sleep hygiene becomes critical: same bedtime, dark room, no screens 1h before

1 WEEK OUT:
  - Routine becomes everything. Same wake time, same meals, same sequence daily.
  - Visualization: 2x daily (morning + before bed)
  - Reduce any new information. Do not learn new techniques.
  - Confidence-building review: watch highlights of your own best performances
  - Positive self-talk: actively replace doubt with evidence-based confidence
  - Prepare fight-day bag, outfit, music playlist, everything pre-decided

DAY BEFORE:
  - Relaxation protocol: progressive muscle relaxation (20 minutes)
  - No training of any kind
  - Watch a movie, spend time with support person, stay calm
  - Prepare everything for fight day (food, clothes, transport, timeline)
  - Sleep: get to bed at normal time. If cannot sleep, still rest (dark, quiet)
  - Reminder: the work is done. Tomorrow is about expression, not preparation.

FIGHT DAY:
  - Wake at planned time (not too early)
  - Morning routine: 10min visualization, light breakfast, light movement
  - 5min shadow boxing (slow, just to feel the body move)
  - Arrive at venue 3 hours before fight time (minimum)
  - Controlled warmup with coach (building from 20% to 70% intensity over 20min)
  - 10min before: activation cues (coach speaks focus words, final breath work)
  - Walk to cage: controlled breathing, focus on first 30 seconds of round 1
  - Cage entry: own the space. Deep breath. When ref says go: EXECUTE.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
4.2 — VISUALIZATION TECHNIQUES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

HOW TO VISUALIZE EFFECTIVELY:
  - Find a quiet, relaxed position (sitting or lying down)
  - Close eyes, take 10 deep breaths to relax fully
  - See in FIRST PERSON (through your own eyes, not watching yourself)
  - Include ALL senses:
    * See: the cage, lights, opponent's stance, referee
    * Hear: crowd, corner calls, breathing, impact sounds
    * Feel: gloves on hands, mat under feet, sweat, heartbeat
    * Smell: the venue, the heat
  - Include emotions: feel the confidence, the controlled aggression
  - Move at REAL TIME speed (not slow motion)
  - Duration: 5-10 minutes per scenario

SCENARIO 1 — PERFECT PERFORMANCE (Domination):
  Visualize: Walk out confident. Touch gloves. From the opening bell, everything
  works. Jab lands clean. Opponent reacts exactly as gameplan predicted. You push
  forward, land the combination. They shoot — you sprawl perfectly, punish them.
  Back to the feet, you continue pressuring. Find the opening, land the finish.
  Referee stops it. You've won convincingly.
  Purpose: builds confidence, rehearses ideal execution.

SCENARIO 2 — ADVERSITY OVERCOME (Resilience):
  Visualize: You get hit hard early. See stars momentarily. Opponent rushes in.
  You clinch, buy time, breathe. Head clears. You work back to distance.
  Start landing your shots. Build momentum. By round 2-3, you've taken over.
  You find the finish despite the rocky start.
  Purpose: Prepares for adversity. Rehearses recovery. Reduces panic if hit.

SCENARIO 3 — LATE FIGHT FINISH (Championship rounds):
  Visualize: Close fight. Going into the final round, you might be down on
  the scorecards. Corner tells you that you need a finish. Deep breath. You
  find another gear. Push the pace. Make the opponent crack under sustained
  pressure. Late in the round, the opening appears. You take it. Finish.
  Purpose: Builds belief in late-fight capacity. Rehearses urgency without panic.

ADDITIONAL SCENARIOS TO VISUALIZE:
  - Getting taken down but sweeping/escaping immediately
  - Clinch battle where you dominate position
  - Executing the exact gameplan combination that scores the knockdown
  - Final 30 seconds of a round where you steal it with late flurry

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
4.3 — CONFIDENCE AFTER A LOSS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

WEEK 1 (IMMEDIATELY AFTER LOSS):
  - Physical rest: no training. Heal body and mind.
  - Do NOT watch the fight back yet. Too emotional, too soon.
  - Support network: spend time with people who care about you as a person, not
    just as a fighter. Family, close friends, partner.
  - Allow emotions: anger, sadness, frustration are all normal. Feel them.
  - Do NOT make any career decisions in this week. No "I'm retiring" or
    "I'm changing camps." Emotions are too raw.
  - Light physical activity only if desired (walk, swim, nothing competitive)

WEEKS 2-3 (OBJECTIVE REVIEW):
  - Watch the fight back with coach. Be analytical, not emotional.
  - Identify SPECIFIC fixable issues. Not "I suck" but "I dropped my hand after
    the jab and got countered." Specific, technical, fixable.
  - Write down 3 concrete things to improve. Only 3.
  - Start to return to the gym: light technique drilling, low pressure
  - No sparring yet. Technical work only.
  - Continue mental health check: if depression, loss of motivation, or avoidance
    symptoms are severe, consider sports psychologist

WEEKS 3-4 (RETURN TO TRAINING):
  - Resume full technical training
  - NO hard sparring yet
  - Light play sparring acceptable (30-40% intensity, with trusted partners)
  - Work specifically on the 3 identified weaknesses from fight review
  - Rebuild confidence through repetition: do the drills until they feel second
    nature again. Confidence = competence felt.

WEEKS 5+ (PROGRESSIVE RETURN):
  - Gradually increase sparring intensity (50% → 70% → 85% over 3-4 weeks)
  - Start with smaller/lesser-experienced partners if confidence is fragile
  - Celebrate small wins: landed a combo clean, defended a takedown, good round
  - Mental reframing work:
    * "A loss is data. It tells me exactly what to fix."
    * "I am not defined by one performance."
    * "Every champion has losses. What matters is the response."
  - Only accept next fight when coach AND fighter agree confidence is genuine

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
4.4 — ANXIETY MANAGEMENT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

BOX BREATHING (Pre-fight anxiety control):
  - Inhale through nose: 4 seconds
  - Hold (lungs full): 4 seconds
  - Exhale through mouth: 4 seconds
  - Hold (lungs empty): 4 seconds
  - Repeat for 2-5 minutes
  - Effect: activates parasympathetic nervous system, reduces cortisol, slows
    heart rate, brings mental clarity

PROGRESSIVE MUSCLE RELAXATION (Night before / pre-fight):
  - Start at feet: tense ALL muscles in feet for 5 seconds, then release.
    Notice the contrast between tension and relaxation.
  - Move up: calves (5s tense, release), thighs, glutes, abdomen, chest,
    hands, arms, shoulders, neck, face.
  - Take 20-30 minutes for full body.
  - Effect: teaches body awareness of tension vs relaxation. Reduces physical
    anxiety symptoms. Excellent for sleep difficulty before fights.

GROUNDING TECHNIQUE (When overwhelmed / pre-fight panic):
  5-4-3-2-1 method:
  - Name 5 things you can SEE (the cage, your gloves, the ref, the lights, the clock)
  - Name 4 things you can TOUCH (floor under feet, gloves on hands, shorts, mouthguard)
  - Name 3 things you can HEAR (crowd, music, corner instructions)
  - Name 2 things you can SMELL (arena, sweat)
  - Name 1 thing you can TASTE (mouthguard, water)
  Effect: pulls attention to present moment, out of anxious future-thinking.

PRE-PERFORMANCE ROUTINE (Ritual for consistency):
  Establish the SAME routine before every training session and every fight:
  - Same music on headphones during warmup
  - Same warmup sequence (skip rope → shadow box → pads → stretch)
  - Same corner team interactions (who speaks, what they say)
  - Same clothing/gear preparation order
  PURPOSE: familiarity = comfort. When anxiety is high, routine provides a
  sense of control. The brain says "I've done this before. I know what comes next."

MANAGING ADRENALINE DUMP:
  The adrenaline dump occurs when excessive nervous energy causes premature
  fatigue in round 1. Heart rate spikes, breathing becomes shallow, muscles
  tense and fatigue quickly.
  Prevention:
  - Thorough warmup before entering the cage (break a light sweat)
  - Breathing: conscious slow exhales in the first 60 seconds of the fight
  - Start at 70% intensity for first 30 seconds (unless gameplan requires blitz)
  - Remind fighter: "The first 2 minutes, just breathe and execute technique.
    Do not chase the knockout. Let it come to you."
  Recovery if it happens:
  - Clinch to buy time and slow pace
  - Breathe: exhale forcefully, inhale naturally
  - Focus on one simple technique (jab only, movement only)
  - Between rounds: long slow exhales, ice on back of neck, sip water

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
4.5 — FIGHT IQ & DECISION MAKING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

WHEN TO PUSH VS WHEN TO RECOVER:
  Push (increase output) when:
  - Opponent is visibly hurt (wobbled, covering up, backing straight up)
  - You just landed a clean shot and opponent hasn't recovered position
  - Final 30 seconds of a round (steal the round / judges' impression)
  - You're behind on scorecards and need urgency
  - Opponent is tired (hands dropping, flat-footed, heavy breathing)

  Recover (reduce output, manage energy) when:
  - You just got hit hard and need to clear your head
  - Heart rate is too high (can't breathe, arms heavy)
  - Early in a round after a very hard previous round
  - You're ahead on scorecards and in a decision fight
  - Opponent is fresh and looking for a counter (don't be reckless)

READING THE SCORECARDS (Knowing your position):
  - Before each fight, assign value to what you do well
  - After each round, honest self-assessment: "Did I win that round?"
  - If you won rounds 1 and 2 in a 3-round fight: you can manage round 3
  - If you lost rounds 1 and 2: you NEED round 3 and may need a finish
  - In a 5-round fight: losing rounds 1-3 means you likely need a finish
  - Corner must communicate honestly: "You need this round" or "Great round, stay smart"
  - Do not assume judges see it your way — if close, treat it as though you're losing

ADAPTING WHEN THE GAMEPLAN FAILS:
  Signs your Plan A is not working:
  - Your primary weapon isn't landing (opponent has figured out timing/range)
  - Opponent is imposing their gameplan successfully
  - You're getting hit by the same shot repeatedly

  What to do:
  - Switch to Plan B (this must be prepared in camp: "If striking isn't working, grapple")
  - Change range: if fighting outside isn't working, close distance (or vice versa)
  - Change level of attack: if head hunting fails, go body
  - Change rhythm: if consistent pace isn't working, add feints and varied timing
  - Communicate with corner: between rounds, listen for adjustments
  - DO NOT abandon structure entirely. Plan B is still a plan. Panic = loss.

MANAGING ADRENALINE DUMP (Round 1 fatigue from nerves):
  - Stay patient in round 1. Do not try to end it immediately (unless that IS the plan)
  - First 60 seconds: jab, move, breathe. Establish range and rhythm.
  - Allow heart rate to settle naturally through controlled breathing
  - Focus on technique execution, not outcome. "Throw the jab correctly" not "knock him out"
  - If fatigue hits early: clinch, lean on opponent, slow the pace, survive to round 2
  - Remember: round 2 and 3 will feel MUCH better once adrenaline normalizes

SECTION 5: NUTRITION FOR FIGHTERS
================================================================================

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
5.1 — BASE NUTRITION (OFF-CAMP / GENERAL TRAINING)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

CALORIC NEEDS:
  Calculation: 35-40 kcal per kilogram of bodyweight for active fighters
  Example: 75kg fighter = 2625-3000 kcal/day
  Adjust based on: training volume, body composition goals, metabolic rate
  Off-camp: eat at maintenance or slight surplus to support recovery and adaptation

MACRONUTRIENT TARGETS:
  Protein: 2-2.2g per kg bodyweight
    Sources: chicken, turkey, fish, lean beef, eggs, whey protein, Greek yogurt
    Purpose: muscle repair, recovery, immune function
    Example (75kg): 150-165g protein/day

  Carbohydrates: 4-6g per kg bodyweight
    Sources: rice, oats, sweet potato, pasta, bread, fruits, vegetables
    Purpose: fuel training, replenish glycogen, brain function
    Example (75kg): 300-450g carbs/day (adjust to training volume)

  Fats: 1-1.2g per kg bodyweight
    Sources: olive oil, nuts, avocado, fatty fish, egg yolks
    Purpose: hormones (testosterone), joint health, vitamin absorption
    Example (75kg): 75-90g fat/day

HYDRATION:
  Minimum: 3-4L water daily (more on heavy training days)
  Signs of good hydration: pale yellow urine, urinating every 2-3 hours
  Add electrolytes on heavy sweat days (sodium, potassium, magnesium)
  Avoid excessive caffeine dehydration (limit to 3-4 cups coffee/day max)

MICRONUTRIENT PRIORITIES:
  Iron: for oxygen transport (red meat 2-3x/week or supplement if vegetarian)
  Zinc: immune function, testosterone (oysters, beef, pumpkin seeds)
  Vitamin D: bone health, immune, mood (sun exposure or supplement)
  B-vitamins: energy metabolism (whole grains, meats, eggs)
  Calcium: bone density (dairy, fortified foods, leafy greens)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
5.2 — IN-CAMP NUTRITION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

WEEKS 1-4: MAINTENANCE CALORIES
  - Eat at maintenance (neither surplus nor deficit)
  - High quality whole foods: minimize processed food
  - Protein priority: hit 2-2.2g/kg every single day
  - Carbs: match to training volume (high training day = high carb)
  - One "relaxed" meal per week if needed psychologically (not a binge)

WEEKS 5-6: SLIGHT DEFICIT (If weight cut required)
  - Reduce by 300-500kcal below maintenance
  - Reduce primarily from carbs and fat (never reduce protein in camp)
  - New targets: Protein 2.2-2.5g/kg, Carbs 3-4g/kg, Fat 0.8-1g/kg
  - Monitor energy: if training quality drops, add 100-200kcal back (from carbs)
  - Weigh daily, track weekly average (expect 0.5-0.8kg/week loss)

WEEK 7: TRANSITION TO CUT
  - Low fiber begins (see weight cut section)
  - Reduce food volume (smaller meals, more calorie-dense choices)
  - Carbs continue dropping: 2-3g/kg
  - Begin water loading protocol
  - Keep protein at 2.5g/kg (preserve muscle during energy deficit)

FIGHT WEEK: SEE WEIGHT CUTTING PROTOCOL (Section 3)

NUTRITION FOR RECOVERY BETWEEN SESSIONS (2-a-day training):
  Between AM and PM session:
  - Fast-digesting protein + carbs within 30min of AM session ending
  - Full meal 2 hours before PM session
  - Example post-AM: protein shake + banana (immediately after)
  - Example pre-PM: 200g rice + 150g chicken + small portion vegetables (2h before)
  Total daily calories may need to increase by 300-500kcal on double-session days.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
5.3 — MEAL TIMING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

PRE-TRAINING (2-3 hours before):
  Goal: Topped up glycogen, steady blood sugar, no GI distress
  Ideal meal: Complex carbs + moderate protein + low fat
  Examples:
    - 80g oats + 1 scoop whey + banana
    - 200g rice + 150g chicken breast + small salad
    - 2 slices toast + 3 eggs + fruit
  Avoid: High fat (slows digestion), high fiber (GI discomfort), dairy (some fighters)

  If training within 60 minutes (insufficient time for full meal):
  - Banana + small protein shake
  - Rice cakes + honey + few almonds
  - Sports drink + protein bar (20g protein max)

INTRA-TRAINING (During sessions >90 minutes):
  - Sip BCAA drink (5-10g BCAAs in water) or sports drink (30g carbs/hour)
  - Particularly important for long sparring or double-session days
  - If session is <60 minutes: water only is fine

POST-TRAINING (Within 30-45 minutes — the anabolic window):
  Goal: Begin recovery immediately. Protein synthesis peaks with stimulus.
  Ideal: 30-40g fast-digesting protein + 40-60g fast carbohydrates
  Examples:
    - Whey protein shake + banana + honey
    - Chocolate milk (400-500ml) — fast, convenient, effective
    - 200g white rice + protein shake
  Then follow with a full whole-food meal within 2 hours.

BEFORE BED (30-60 minutes before sleep):
  Goal: Sustained amino acid delivery overnight (8h without food)
  Ideal: Slow-digesting protein + small amount of healthy fat
  Examples:
    - 200g cottage cheese (casein protein: slow release)
    - Casein protein shake + tablespoon peanut butter
    - Greek yogurt + handful of nuts
  Avoid: Large carb-heavy meals before bed (blood sugar disruption to sleep)

FIGHT DAY NUTRITION: (See rehydration protocol in Section 3.4 for post-weigh-in)
  - Eat familiar foods only. Nothing new or experimental.
  - Last full meal: 3-4 hours before fight
  - Last snack: 1-2 hours before (banana, rice cakes, small gummy bears)
  - Caffeine: if used, take 60-90 min before fight time (3-5mg/kg)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
5.4 — SUPPLEMENTS (LEGAL & EVIDENCE-BASED)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

CREATINE MONOHYDRATE:
  Dose: 3-5g daily (no loading phase necessary, though 20g/day for 5 days speeds saturation)
  Benefits: +5-10% strength, improved power output, faster recovery between sets,
    neuroprotective (relevant for fighters taking head impact)
  Timing: any time of day (consistency > timing)
  Note: Causes 1-3kg water weight gain (intramuscular). Stop 2-3 weeks before
    weigh-in if water weight is a concern. Resume post-weigh-in immediately.
  Safety: Most studied supplement in history. No kidney damage in healthy individuals.

CAFFEINE:
  Dose: 3-6mg/kg bodyweight, taken 30-60 minutes before training
  Benefits: Improved power output, reaction time, pain tolerance, focus
  Example: 75kg fighter = 225-450mg caffeine (2-4 cups coffee equivalent)
  Note: Build tolerance gradually. Do not exceed 6mg/kg. Avoid after 2pm (sleep).
  Competition: Legal in all sports at these doses.

OMEGA-3 FATTY ACIDS (Fish Oil):
  Dose: 2-3g EPA+DHA combined daily
  Benefits: Reduces inflammation, joint health, brain health (concussion
    protection potentially), heart health, recovery
  Timing: With meals (fat aids absorption)
  Source: Fish oil capsules or liquid, or algae-based for plant-based fighters

VITAMIN D3:
  Dose: 2000-4000 IU daily (or based on blood test — target 50-70 ng/mL)
  Benefits: Bone health, immune function, muscle function, mood regulation
  Note: Most fighters training indoors are deficient. Test blood levels annually.
  Timing: With a meal containing fat

MAGNESIUM:
  Dose: 400-600mg before bed (glycinate or bisglycinate form preferred)
  Benefits: Sleep quality, muscle relaxation, cramp prevention, stress reduction
  Note: Most athletes are deficient. Critical during weight cuts.
  Avoid: Magnesium oxide (poor absorption, causes GI issues)

BETA-ALANINE:
  Dose: 3.2-6.4g daily (split into 2 doses to reduce tingling sensation)
  Benefits: Buffers hydrogen ions during high-intensity work (delays fatigue),
    particularly useful for sustained grappling exchanges and later rounds
  Timing: Daily (cumulative effect over 4+ weeks, timing within day doesn't matter)
  Note: Causes harmless tingling (paresthesia) at higher single doses

OTHER WORTHWHILE SUPPLEMENTS:
  - Zinc: 15-30mg/day if not getting enough from food (immune, testosterone)
  - Tart cherry juice: 30ml concentrate 2x daily (reduces DOMS, anti-inflammatory)
  - Collagen peptides: 10-15g/day (joint/tendon health, combine with vitamin C)
  - Ashwagandha: 300-600mg/day (cortisol management, recovery — some evidence)

SUPPLEMENTS TO AVOID:
  - Testosterone boosters (unproven, potentially contaminated)
  - Fat burners (stimulant overload, no magic for weight loss)
  - Any supplement not third-party tested (contamination risk = failed drug test)
  - ALWAYS use NSF Certified for Sport or Informed Sport certified products

SECTION 6: COMMON INJURIES & REHABILITATION
================================================================================

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
6.1 — HAND & WRIST INJURIES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

BOXER'S FRACTURE (5th Metacarpal):
  Mechanism: Improper fist formation on impact, usually hitting with ring/pinky knuckle
  Symptoms: Immediate pain over outer hand, swelling, unable to make fist
  Timeline: 4-6 weeks in cast/splint, then 2-4 weeks rehabilitation
  Rehab: Grip strengthening (rice bucket), wrist curls, progressive bag work
  Return to full training: 8-10 weeks total
  Prevention: Proper wrapping technique (figure-8 around knuckles, wrist support),
    correct fist alignment (land with index and middle knuckle), gradual heavy bag introduction

WRIST SPRAINS:
  Mechanism: Hyperextension during impact, catching with open hand, posting on mat
  Grades: I (mild stretch), II (partial tear), III (complete ligament rupture)
  Timeline: Grade I: 1-2 weeks. Grade II: 3-6 weeks. Grade III: 6-12 weeks or surgery.
  Rehab: Wrist curls/extensions, radial/ulnar deviation, pronation/supination
  Prevention: Strong wrist extensors, proper wrapping, wrist guards for grappling

METACARPOPHALANGEAL JOINT SPRAINS (Knuckle injuries):
  Mechanism: Repeated impact, hyperextension of fingers during grappling
  Timeline: 2-6 weeks depending on severity
  Management: Buddy taping, ice, anti-inflammatory, modified training
  Prevention: Proper glove fit, hand strengthening, grip work

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
6.2 — KNEE INJURIES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

ACL TEAR (Anterior Cruciate Ligament):
  Mechanism: Planting and twisting (common during scrambles, spinning kicks,
    awkward takedown landings), hyperextension, valgus collapse
  Symptoms: Pop heard/felt, immediate swelling, instability, inability to pivot
  Treatment: Surgical reconstruction (patellar tendon, hamstring, or quadriceps graft)
  Timeline: 9-12 months return to full contact (some fighters take 14-18 months)
  Rehab phases:
    - Weeks 1-6: ROM recovery, swelling management, quad activation, gentle leg raises
    - Weeks 6-12: Progressive strengthening, stationary bike, bodyweight squats
    - Months 3-6: Full strength training, light jogging, balance/proprioception
    - Months 6-9: Sport-specific movement, agility, light drilling
    - Months 9-12: Progressive sparring, full return when functional tests passed
  Prevention: Hamstring/glute strengthening, proper landing mechanics (knee over toe,
    avoid valgus), proprioception training (single-leg balance, wobble board),
    neuromuscular training (cutting drills with proper form)

MCL SPRAIN (Medial Collateral Ligament):
  Mechanism: Lateral force to outside of knee, twisting during grappling,
    leg lock defenses, inside knee-on-knee pressure
  Grades: I (2-3 weeks), II (4-6 weeks), III (6-8 weeks, rarely surgical)
  Treatment: Usually conservative (brace, rehab, progressive loading)
  Prevention: Lateral hip strengthening, avoid full knee extension under load in twisting

MENISCUS TEARS:
  Mechanism: Deep squat + twist (very common in grappling transitions, guard play)
  Types: Lateral vs Medial, Bucket handle vs Radial vs Longitudinal
  Treatment:
    - Small tears / degenerative: conservative (rest, rehab, 4-8 weeks)
    - Large tears / locking: arthroscopic surgery (repair or partial removal)
    - Repair: 3-6 months recovery. Removal: 4-8 weeks recovery.
  Prevention: Maintain deep squat mobility, avoid training through knee catching/locking

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
6.3 — SHOULDER INJURIES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

ROTATOR CUFF STRAIN:
  Mechanism: Repeated overhead work (elbows from top position), kimura/americana
    stress, direct impact on shoulder during fall, overuse from punching
  Symptoms: Pain with overhead movement, weakness in external rotation, night pain
  Timeline: 4-8 weeks for strain. If partial tear: 8-16 weeks. Full tear: surgery.
  Rehab: External rotation with band, prone Y-T-W, wall slides, scapular retraction
  Return criteria: Full pain-free ROM, strength within 90% of uninjured side
  Prevention: Daily external rotation exercises (band pull-aparts, face pulls),
    balanced pressing to pulling ratio (2:1 pull to push volume minimum)

LABRAL TEAR (SLAP or Bankart):
  Mechanism: Shoulder dislocation, repeated subluxation, falling on outstretched hand,
    kimura/americana (arm forcefully rotated while caught in submission)
  Symptoms: Clicking, catching, deep shoulder pain, instability
  Treatment: Conservative first (rehab 8-12 weeks). If unstable/recurring: surgery (repair or debridement)
  Timeline: Conservative: 3-4 months. Post-surgery: 6-9 months for full MMA return.
  Prevention: Strong rotator cuff, scapular stability, tap EARLY to submissions (ego kills shoulders)

SHOULDER DISLOCATION/SUBLUXATION:
  Mechanism: Arm forced into external rotation + abduction (kimura position),
    posting on arm during takedown, reaching back to prevent takedown
  First dislocation: 6 weeks immobilization + 6-8 weeks rehab
  Recurrent: May require Bankart repair surgery
  WARNING: Recurrence rate is HIGH (>50% in athletes under 25). After first
  dislocation, rigorous rehab and prevention work is mandatory.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
6.4 — CONCUSSIONS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

DEFINITION: Traumatic brain injury caused by acceleration/deceleration forces
or direct impact to the head. Does NOT require loss of consciousness.

SYMPTOMS:
  - Headache, pressure in head
  - Dizziness, balance problems
  - Nausea/vomiting
  - Confusion, feeling "foggy"
  - Sensitivity to light/noise
  - Memory problems (can't remember what happened)
  - Emotional changes (irritability, sadness)
  - Sleep disturbance

MINIMUM PROTOCOL:
  - Minimum 2 weeks ZERO contact after ANY concussion (many experts say 4 weeks)
  - This includes no sparring, no grappling with head contact, no heading balls, nothing

RETURN TO PLAY PROTOCOL (Graduated):
  Stage 1 (Day 3+ if symptom-free at rest): Light aerobic (walking, stationary bike <70% max HR)
    - If symptoms return: stop, rest another 24-48h, try again
  Stage 2 (if Stage 1 completed without symptoms): Sport-specific cardio (drilling at 70-80%)
    - No contact, no impact risk
  Stage 3: Non-contact training drills (pad work at moderate intensity, grappling drill without live)
  Stage 4: Light contact practice (controlled sparring, 50% power, with trusted partners)
  Stage 5: Full contact practice (normal sparring intensity)
  Stage 6: Competition (full fight)

  EACH STAGE MINIMUM 1 WEEK. If symptoms return at any stage, go back 2 stages.
  Total minimum return time: 6 weeks from concussion to competition (and often longer).

CRITICAL RULES:
  - NEVER return same day after a concussion. NEVER.
  - NEVER "push through" concussion symptoms. This causes Second Impact Syndrome
    (catastrophic brain swelling, potentially fatal).
  - If loss of consciousness occurred: minimum 4 weeks no contact.
  - If fighter has had 3+ concussions in 12 months: MANDATORY extended break
    (3-6 months minimum). Consider career implications seriously.
  - Multiple concussions cause CUMULATIVE damage. CTE is real. Long-term brain
    health must be prioritized over any single fight.

PREVENTION:
  - Strong neck muscles (neck curls, manual resistance, harness work) reduce
    concussion risk by controlling head acceleration
  - Mouthguard (properly fitted custom guard is best) — may reduce concussion severity
  - Defensive skill development (don't get hit is the best prevention)
  - Reduce hard sparring frequency (2x/week MAX, many elite camps do 1x/week)
  - Consider headgear for training (debated, but may reduce cuts and superficial damage)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
6.5 — BACK (LUMBAR SPINE) INJURIES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

DISC HERNIATION / BULGE:
  Mechanism: Heavy deadlift with flexed spine, explosive rotation under load,
    defending stacking passes in guard, bridging repeatedly
  Symptoms: Low back pain radiating to leg (sciatica), numbness/tingling,
    weakness in leg, pain worse sitting or bending forward
  Treatment:
    - Conservative first (90% resolve without surgery): 6-12 weeks
    - Physical therapy: McKenzie method, core stability, nerve gliding
    - Avoid flexion-based movements initially
    - If no improvement after 12 weeks, or progressive neurological deficit: surgery
  Surgery recovery: 3-6 months for discectomy, 6-12 months for fusion
  Prevention: Perfect deadlift/squat form (neutral spine ALWAYS), strong core
    (brace before loading), hip mobility (so spine doesn't compensate)

MUSCLE STRAIN (Erector Spinae / QL):
  Mechanism: Twisting under load, explosive movement without warmup, accumulated
    fatigue from grappling
  Timeline: 2-4 weeks for mild strain, 4-8 weeks for moderate
  Treatment: Initial rest (2-3 days), then progressive loading. Ice first 48h,
    then heat. Light movement is better than complete immobility.
  Prevention: Core stability training (anti-rotation: Pallof press, anti-extension:
    dead bugs, anti-lateral flexion: suitcase carries), adequate warmup before
    explosive movement

PREVENTION STRATEGIES FOR BACK HEALTH:
  - McGill Big 3: Curl-up, Side plank, Bird dog — daily
  - Hip hinge pattern mastery before any heavy deadlifting
  - Thoracic mobility: if upper back is stiff, lower back compensates
  - Core bracing before ANY loaded movement or explosive action
  - Avoid loaded spinal flexion + rotation (the injury mechanism for most disc herniations)
  - Decompress after training: hang from bar (30-60s), child's pose, supine twist

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
6.6 — RETURNING FROM ANY INJURY
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

GOLDEN RULES:
  1. Never rush. Reinjury rate is 3x higher if return is premature.
  2. The injured area must be STRONGER than before injury (prehab > pre-injury level)
  3. Pain-free does NOT mean healed. Tissue remodeling continues long after pain resolves.
  4. If it was surgical: follow surgeon's timeline. Do not freelance.
  5. Communicate honestly with coaching staff about how it feels (no "toughing it out")

FUNCTIONAL TESTS BEFORE RETURN:
  - Single-leg hop test: >90% of uninjured side
  - Change of direction test: full speed without hesitation or guarding
  - Sport-specific movement test: can perform ALL required movements without pain
  - Strength test: >90% of uninjured side (or pre-injury level for bilateral)
  - Psychological readiness: fighter must feel CONFIDENT, not protecting the area.
    If they're subconsciously guarding, they're not ready (and will likely compensate
    and injure something else).

PROGRESSIVE LOADING PROTOCOL:
  Stage 1 — 50% (1-2 weeks):
    Light drilling, technical work only. No live resistance. No speed.
    Purpose: Reintroduce movement patterns, build tissue tolerance.

  Stage 2 — 70% (1-2 weeks):
    Moderate drilling with cooperative partner. Moderate speed.
    Light rolling/sparring with VERY trusted partners who know the injury.
    Purpose: Increase load on tissue. Begin sport-specific stress.

  Stage 3 — 85% (1-2 weeks):
    Near-full intensity with controlled sparring partners.
    No competition-level exchanges yet. Can train most things.
    Purpose: Final preparation for full return. Build confidence.

  Stage 4 — 100% (1+ weeks before competition):
    Full training, full sparring, no restrictions.
    If this stage goes well for 2+ weeks: cleared for competition.
    Purpose: Confirm readiness. Build full confidence.

SECTION 7: RULES & SCORING (UNIFIED RULES OF MMA)
================================================================================

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
7.1 — UNIFIED RULES OF MMA
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

LEGAL TECHNIQUES:
  Striking:
    - Punches (all types: jab, cross, hook, uppercut, overhand, spinning backfist)
    - Kicks (all types: low kick, body kick, head kick, front kick, spinning kicks)
    - Knees (to body and head of STANDING opponent; rules vary for grounded opponent)
    - Elbows (all angles EXCEPT 12-to-6 downward elbow striking motion)
  Grappling:
    - All takedowns (single leg, double leg, trips, throws, slams within rules)
    - All submissions (chokes, joint locks, muscle locks)
    - Ground and pound (strikes from top position to legal target areas)
    - Clinch work (dirty boxing, knees, trips, cage work)

ILLEGAL TECHNIQUES (Fouls):
    - Strikes to the back of the head (the "mohawk" area from crown to base of skull)
    - Strikes to the spine directly
    - Groin strikes (immediate point deduction if egregious, warning first usually)
    - Knees to a downed opponent's head (ONE Championship allows this, UFC does not)
      NOTE: "Downed" = any body part other than soles of feet touching the mat
      (updated in some jurisdictions: now requires a full palm down, not just fingertips)
    - 12-to-6 elbows (straight downward motion, perpendicular to ground)
    - Eye gouging / eye poking (intentional = DQ, accidental = warning/time)
    - Fish-hooking (inserting fingers in mouth, nose, or ears and pulling)
    - Fence/cage grabbing (immediate warning, point deduction if repeated/advantageous)
    - Grabbing shorts or gloves of opponent
    - Small joint manipulation (cannot grab individual fingers/toes and bend them)
    - Biting
    - Headbutting
    - Strikes to throat (direct punch to trachea)
    - Spiking opponent on head/neck (pile-driver motion)
    - Hair pulling
    - Attacking during break (when referee separates fighters)
    - Timidity / avoiding contact / running (can be warned/penalized)
    - Using abusive language in the cage
    - Interference from corner

ROUND STRUCTURE:
  Non-title / Non-main event: 3 rounds x 5 minutes, with 1 minute rest between
  Main event or Title fight: 5 rounds x 5 minutes, with 1 minute rest between
  NOTE: Some promotions (ONE Championship, Rizin) use different round structures.

WEIGHT CLASS LIMITS (UFC Standard):
  Strawweight: 115 lbs (52.2 kg)
  Flyweight: 125 lbs (56.7 kg)
  Bantamweight: 135 lbs (61.2 kg)
  Featherweight: 145 lbs (65.8 kg)
  Lightweight: 155 lbs (70.3 kg)
  Welterweight: 170 lbs (77.1 kg)
  Middleweight: 185 lbs (83.9 kg)
  Light Heavyweight: 205 lbs (93.0 kg)
  Heavyweight: 265 lbs (120.2 kg)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
7.2 — JUDGING CRITERIA (IN ORDER OF IMPORTANCE)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The Unified Rules establish a HIERARCHY of scoring criteria:

1. EFFECTIVE STRIKING AND GRAPPLING (Primary Criterion):
   Effective Striking:
     - Judged by DAMAGE first, then significant strikes, then volume
     - A strike that visibly hurts the opponent > 10 strikes that don't
     - Knockdowns are valued very highly
     - Strikes that change the complexion of the fight carry more weight
     - Body shots and leg kicks that accumulate damage count significantly

   Effective Grappling:
     - Successful takedowns (finishing in dominant position)
     - Submission attempts that threaten the opponent
     - Advancing position (guard → half guard → side control → mount → back)
     - Ground and pound from dominant positions
     - Reversal from bottom to top
     - A takedown that leads to nothing is worth less than a takedown with follow-up

2. EFFECTIVE AGGRESSIVENESS (Secondary — used only if striking/grappling is equal):
   - Moving forward with purpose and intent to finish
   - Pushing the pace of the fight
   - Attempting to end the fight (not just accumulating safe strikes)
   - NOTE: Aggressive but ineffective (swinging wildly and missing) does NOT score
   - Effective counterstriking while moving backward CAN outscore forward pressure

3. CAGE/OCTAGON CONTROL (Tertiary — used only if all above are equal):
   - Dictating WHERE the fight takes place
   - Dictating the PACE of the fight
   - Forcing opponent to fight your fight (making them react to you)
   - Ring generalship and positioning
   - This is the tiebreaker and rarely decides a round on its own

IMPORTANT: Judges score PER ROUND. Previous rounds do not influence the current
round's score. Each round starts fresh at 10-10 and is scored independently.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
7.3 — 10-POINT MUST SYSTEM
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

STANDARD ROUND (10-9):
  - The winner of the round receives 10 points
  - The loser receives 9 points
  - Most rounds are scored 10-9
  - Close rounds where both fighters had success: still 10-9 (must give winner 10)

10-8 ROUND (Dominant round):
  A 10-8 round should be awarded when:
  - Near finish: One fighter was close to being stopped (hurt badly, almost
    submitted, referee was close to stopping)
  - OR Complete domination: One-sided striking (significant damage differential)
    or one-sided grappling (complete control with damage/threat throughout)
  - Best practice: BOTH near finish + domination = definite 10-8
  - Just one element (near finish OR domination): judges may or may not give 10-8

  Under current rules interpretation, 10-8 rounds should be given more frequently
  than they were historically. A clearly one-sided round SHOULD be 10-8.

10-7 ROUND (Extremely rare):
  - Both a near finish AND complete domination to an extreme degree
  - Examples: Multiple knockdowns + sustained ground and pound with opponent
    offering no offense for essentially the entire round
  - Extremely rare. Maybe a handful exist in major promotion history.

10-10 ROUND (Draw round):
  - Only awarded when the round is so close that NO winner can be identified
  - Extremely rare in practice (judges are instructed to pick a winner)
  - Both fighters performed equally in ALL criteria

HOW FINAL SCORES WORK:
  - 3-round fight: each judge's scores for all 3 rounds are totaled
  - Example: Judge scores 10-9, 9-10, 10-9 = 29-28 for Fighter A
  - 5-round fight: total across all 5 rounds
  - Three judges score independently

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
7.4 — WAYS TO WIN
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

KNOCKOUT (KO):
  Fighter renders opponent unconscious through strikes. Referee stops the bout
  when a fighter cannot intelligently defend themselves and is unconscious or
  clearly unable to continue.

TECHNICAL KNOCKOUT (TKO):
  Sub-categories:
  - Strikes: Referee stops because fighter is taking unanswered damage and is no
    longer intelligently defending (even if still conscious)
  - Doctor Stoppage: Ringside physician determines a fighter cannot safely
    continue (usually due to cuts around the eyes affecting vision)
  - Corner Stoppage: Fighter's corner throws in the towel or verbally stops the fight
  - Body Shot TKO: Fighter cannot continue due to body damage (liver shot, etc.)

SUBMISSION:
  Types:
  - Tap (physical): Fighter taps opponent or mat with hand/foot
  - Verbal tap: Fighter verbally says "tap" or screams
  - Referee stoppage: Fighter goes unconscious from choke or joint is clearly in
    danger and fighter cannot respond
  - Technical submission: Fighter is so compromised in a submission (arm trapped,
    unconscious from blood choke) that referee stops it

DECISION:
  - Unanimous Decision (UD): All 3 judges score for the same fighter
    (Example: 29-28, 30-27, 29-28 all for Fighter A)
  - Split Decision (SD): 2 judges score for one fighter, 1 judge for the other
    (Example: 29-28 A, 28-29 B, 29-28 A — Fighter A wins split decision)
  - Majority Decision (MD): 2 judges score for one fighter, 1 judge scores draw
    (Example: 29-28 A, 29-29, 29-28 A — Fighter A wins majority decision)

DRAW:
  - Split Draw: 1 judge scores for A, 1 for B, 1 scores draw
  - Majority Draw: 2 judges score draw, 1 scores for a fighter
  - Unanimous Draw: All 3 judges score it a draw (extremely rare)

NO CONTEST (NC):
  - Accidental foul stops the fight before completion of enough rounds
    (Usually before round 3 in a 5-round fight, or before round 2 in a 3-round fight)
  - Overturned result due to failed drug test (changes to NC retroactively)
  - Both fighters commit simultaneous illegal actions making continuation impossible

DISQUALIFICATION (DQ):
  - Intentional foul: fighter deliberately commits illegal action (biting, eye gouging)
  - Repeated fouls after point deductions: if a fighter continues fouling after
    multiple warnings and point deductions, referee may disqualify
  - Extremely rare in modern MMA (most fouls are handled with warnings/point deductions)

POINT DEDUCTIONS:
  - Referee can deduct 1 point for fouls (after warning usually, though immediate
    deduction is allowed for egregious fouls)
  - A point deduction affects the round score for that round only
  - Example: Fighter A would win round 10-9, but had 1 point deducted = 9-9 round
  - Multiple point deductions in one round are possible
  - Typical progression: verbal warning → warning with point threat → point deduction → DQ

SCORING STRATEGY IMPLICATIONS FOR FIGHTERS:
  - If you think you're winning on points: fight smart, don't take unnecessary risks
  - If you think you're losing on points: increase output, seek finish
  - In a 5-round fight: you can afford to lose a round if you win 3
  - 10-8 rounds can swing a fight: one dominant round can overcome losing 2 close rounds
  - Finish rate correlates with championship success: don't rely solely on decisions
  - In close fights: activity in final 30 seconds of a round influences judges heavily
    (recency bias is real)
